About the Project

The World Integrated Trade Solution (WITS) website provides access to international merchandise trade, tariff and non-tariff measures (NTM) data. However, the presentation of aggregate data on this site is not very intuitive. Our team saw this as an opportunity to use World Bank data and present that information in a way that is more accessible to a general audience while continuing to maintain the original integrity of the WITS website.

Our team, composed of Paul Bernert, Hans Engelbrecht and Sam Ettenson, welcome you to explore this site and explore the different methods we chose for presenting international trade data.

Methodology

Step One: ETL

The data used in this project comes from the WITS website, where they have two CSVs that we used for the entirety of this project (Titled: "Countries at a Glance" and "Country Summaries"). After extracting the data, we used Python (in particular, the Pandas library) to transform the data to get it prepared for loading into our database. These transformations included category renaming, altering the titles of countries (i.e. "United States" to "United States of America"), and other minor changes. The categories of data that were relevant for the respective graphs were then sub-selected out, and a brand new dataframe of relevant content was created. That database was then loaded into Postgres, leading us to the next step.

Step Two: Initialize Database

Once the data was loaded into Postgres, we needed to create a connection between our Postgres database and the server where this website would be created--Heroku. This turned out to be a very challenging and time-consuming task, as initializing a database within Heroku was not intuitive to our group despite reviewing the documentation. However, with this step completed, we were finally able to move on to the final major step.

Step Three: Data Analytics

Each member of the team focused on a different set of analysis. Paul Bernert was responsible for taking relevant data and creating map visualizations that best represent global phenomenon. Hans Engelbrecht was responsible for creating pie/doughnut charts that represent a country's import and export behavior for both macro- and micro-categories. Sam Ettenson was responsible for creating visualizations that represent time-series data (data across a spectrum of time), opting for a line chart to show how countries change over time.
